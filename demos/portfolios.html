Misleading Graphs in Advertisements:

What you see is not always the truth

Statistics are a great way to visualize and represent data.

You must be familiar with graphs and charts like bar graph, line graph, pie chart etc because of the fact that how often they are used in many fields including in Advertisements.

But before we go further and justify the heading we have put on top of the slide, let’s play a little game. Hiding the context (Cherry Picking):

You see, how taking into account a context on which the graph is based on, shapes your decision! Companies and advertisers often play with these contexts to misrepresent the statistics and promote their product without actually lying... The graph above shows you the number of car accidents in both cities, but it does not take into account the population of both the cities. This selection of handful evidences (disregarding the other factors involved) to advertise a product is called cherry picking. An example of misleading advertisement of this kind is as follows:

    These types of graphs are often used to falsely argue that climate change is reversing and the earth’s temperature is decreasing! But the graph above does not take into account the fact that climate change is a long term process and thus can’t be demonstrated by few years (1998-2000). Thus it’s important that we look at the context before blindly believing advertisements.

Manipulation of the scales:

Q) Can you figure out what’s wrong with the graph in the advertisement given aside?

More than 98% of all Chevy trucks sold in the last 10 years are still on the road. Looks impressive, right? Now, let us have a closer look at it. When we see this kind of graph, we automatically think that sizes of the bars are proportionate to their values. So, visually Chevy’s bar looks almost twice as big as Toyota’s. But if you look carefully you will notice that the Y axis starts from 95%, so the percentage for Toyota is just two percent lower than the Chevy trucks! These are called truncated graphs.

Graph is just a tool which helps us to represents data. It depends on how it is used. As, consumer it’s important for us to be aware and look at the context and numbers in the graph before coming to a conclusion.

1. Absence of important details in Advertisement:

Aviation Company Air Asia was fined by the Malaysian Aviation Commission because as you can see in the advertisement it claimed its flights to be free but they did not include other taxes and charges which were charged. Under the MACPC regulations, an airline must indicate the final price of airfares to be paid to the airlines, including taxes, fees, charges and surcharges that are unavoidable at the time the advertisement is put up.
